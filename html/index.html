ABOUT THE EGBEMA KINGDOM

Rooted in the Niger Delta, shaped by the flow of Oil and ancestral traditions

The Egbema kingdom, located in Imo and Rivers states, is an ancient, oil-rich, primarily Igbo-speaking community (specifically 16 villages) with deep roots in the Niger Delta. Historically linked to early Portuguese trade (c. 1445), it became a crucial trading hub, now heavily impacted by oil exploitation, pollution, and administrative division.

Origins and History

  • Migration and Location: The Egbema people in Imo/Rivers states have historical links to, or migrations from, older Delta regions, with traditions referring to early trade with Europeans. The kingdom was divided on Feb 3, 1976, leaving 13 villages in the Ohaji/Egbema LGA of Imo State and 3 in Ogba/Ndoni/Egbema LGA of Rivers State.
  • Trade and Commerce: Historically, Egbema served as a middleman in trade with European merchants in the 15th-17th centuries, supplying goods to the Bini region.
  • Cultural Identity: The Egbema are traditionally considered an Igbo-speaking community or a distinct ethnic group with cultural links to the Niger Delta, sometimes distinguishing themselves from the Ijaw Egbema group in Delta State.

Modern Era and Oil Exploration

  • Oil Discovery: Egbema is a major oil-producing area with significant oil and gas deposits.
  • Marginalization and Conflict: Despite being a major oil producer, the region reports marginalization, lack of infrastructure, and ecological devastation (air, water, land pollution).
  • Administration: The Egbema King (Nze Obi of Egbema) traditionally oversees his subjects in both Imo and Rivers States.

Location and Cultural Roots

Egbema spans Imo and Rivers States, sharing a unified ancestry despite being split by political boundaries in 1976. They are primarily a sub-dialect of the Igbo language (Igboid) group located in the Niger Delta hinterland, with a history influenced by early interactions with European traders. They are distinct from the Ijaw Egbema in Delta/Edo states.

Origins and Ancestral Roots

  • Location: In Imo State (Ohaji/Egbema LGA) and Rivers State (Ogba/Egbema/Ndoni LGA)
  • Ethnicity: Closely linked to Ogba, Egi, and Ndoni groups.
  • Trade and Economy: Traditionally involved in farming and fishing, navigating rivers and creeks.
  • Language: A sub-dialect of Igbo, with strong linguistic links.

Environmental and Socio-economic Challenges

Oil exploitation has brought significant environmental degradation and socioeconomic issues, including pollution and marginalization despite the region's rich resources.

Egbema Oil kingdom spans the rich flood lains of Imo and Rivers States, a major oil-producing region with deep ancestral roots. Our 16 autonomous communities maintain traditional governance system (Nze Obi) while collectively fostering development and cultural preservation.

Since the discovery of commercial oil in the late 1956s, Egbema has contributed over 5% of Nigeria's petroleum output. Yet our identity remains tied to the land, water, and festivals that celebrates our ancestors. We are stewards of both black Gold and living heritage.

Cultures & Traditions

Masquerade festivals, sacred water spirits,and the rhythm of the talking drum defines Egbema identity.

From the annual Alinso Festival that celebrates ancestral reverence to vibrant wrestling contests showcasing young warriors, our culture pulsates with life. Traditional marriage rites, folks music, and the iconic Egbema-Igbo cuisine (spicy fish pepper soup, palm wine) creates a rich tapestry.

HISTORY OF EGBEMA OIL KINGDOM

The Egbema kingdom, an ancient community in Nigeria's Niger Delta, is a major oil-producing region spanning Edo, Delta, Imo, and Rivers States. Historically, the Egbema settled between the 15th and 17th centuries, engaging in farming, fishing, and trade. Oil was discovered in commercial quantities in the late 1950s, making it a critical hub for Nigeria's petroleum Industry, currently contributing a significant portion of national production.

The Oil Economy And Modern Impact

Discovery: Commercial oil exploration began in the late 1950s, profoundly changing the economy from traditional Agriculture.
Production: The area is home to over forty producing oil wells operated by multinationals like Shell, Agip, and Total Energy.
Impact: While oil has boosted national revenue, the community has faced challenges related to environmental degradation and the need for infrastructure development.

Geopolitical Dissection

It is important to distinguish between two main "Egbema" entities: 1. Ijaw Egbema (Edo/Delta): Traditionally located along the Benin River in Edo State, governed by the Agedagba. 2. Igbo Egbema (Imo/Rivers): located within the Ogba/Egbema/Ndoni local government Area of Rivers and Ohaji/Egbema local government Area of Imo. The Egbema people continue to be part of the Niger Delta's and by extension, Nigeria's economic history.

Egbema (Igbo Riverine Clan)

Riverine Igbo clan located in the lower Niger flood plains across Imo and Rivers States, comprising 16 traditional communities (Villages) known for oil and gas production. The major communities often under Nze Obi Leader include Mmahu, Abacheke, Obiakpu, Etekwuru, Obokofia, Ofuruola, Umuorji, Ekugba, Mgbara, Umudike, Obeakpu, Opuoma, Abaezi, Okwuzi, Mgbede, and Aggah. The communities are situated in a region with high oil production and agriculture activity, with some parts falling under Ohaji/Egbema LGA of Imo State and others in Ogba/Egbema/Ndoni LGA of Rivers State.

Egbema Alinso: Also known as Ohaji/Egbema, established in 1976 in Imo State, is an oil-rich region formed from the old Owerri and Ogboko divisions, predominantly inhabited by the Igbo ethnic group, known as a major economic hub. It is historically characterized by agricultural productivity (Adapalm) and massive oil/gas resources, though often cited as politically marginalized.

Key Historic Aspects

Establishment & Geography: The local government was created in 1976. The Egbema Clan was split during this period with some villages (Okwuzi, Mgbede, Aggah) becoming part of Rivers State while the majority remained in Imo State.

Economic Significance

It is a major contributor to Nigeria's economy, housing major oil and gas installations such as the Assa North- Ohaji South project and the Mmahu Power plant in 2006.

Cultural Background

Originally known as "Alinso" (Sacred Land), Egbema is traditionally governed by the Nze Obi, while Ohaji has its own distinct cultural structure.

Administrative & Development Issues

Despite its wealth, the region has historically felt underdeveloped, leading to advocacy for better infrastructure and a greater share of the 13% derivation funds for host communities managed by ISOPADEC. The area remains a critical, yet sometimes neglected, economic hub.
